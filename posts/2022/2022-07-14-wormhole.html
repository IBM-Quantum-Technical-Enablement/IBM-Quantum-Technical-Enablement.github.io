Going down the Wormhole#

Recently our Partners over at Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (LBNL) published an interesting paper on probing quantum gravity in the lab: arXiv:2205.14081. This paper caught our eye for two reasons. First, it is about teleportation via wormholes, so why not check it out? Second, it compares our quantum systems verses the trapped-ion systems from Quantinuum. From a competitive standpoint, these types of papers are fun to look at and see if we can do better. Trying to do so is the basis for this notebook. If you want all the details you will need to see the paper, however the basics can be gathered from Figure 1 in the paper:

Basically they are creating entangled pairs of qubits at the beginning; a prerequisite of all teleportation procedures. Doing evolution backward and forward by the same amout of time on the L group, coupling the L and R groups of qubits with a tunable phase g, and then performing the forward evolution again on the R group of qubits, and then measuring the state on the bottom qubit. As a function of the coupling g the expectation value of the Z operator on the last qubit in both simulation and on the real quantum hardware is seen to be:

We see the signal oscillates as a function of g with the minimum “signal” at \(g/2\pi=0.25\). Quantinuum gets close to 80% of this value while IBM Quantum obtains at most ~35% with error mitigation turned on, and 20% with it off on the ibmq_montreal Falcon R4 system. Our goal is to see if we can do better.

Now, given that this experiment is only 6 qubits it is trivial to simulate, and it is best to verify that we see what we want. So first I will bind the values of g that I am interested in to the circuit. Here I will vary g over \(2\pi\):

g_vals = np.linspace(0, np.pi, 11)

ideal_circs = [qc.bind_parameters({g:val}) for val in g_vals]

Next we get our simulator instance and run the circuits:

sim = Aer.get_backend('aer_simulator')

shots = 10000
ideal_counts = sim.run(ideal_circs, shots=shots).result().get_counts()

The answer is encoded in the expectation value of Z on qubit 5. There is however no good way of computing expectation values in Qiskit. In this case it is a single qubit, and we can extract what we want easily:

ideal_expval = [-1 * cnts.get('1', 0)/shots + cnts.get('0', 0)/shots for cnts in ideal_counts]

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), ideal_expval, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='ideal')
ax.legend()
ax.set_xlabel('$g/2\pi$');

So far so good. Our circuits indeed do what we want them to. Now we can get to doing noisy simulations to see what we should expect.

They targeted the ibmq_montreal system, so lets do the same. We first grab the backend, and then make a noisy simulator mimicking that system:

backend = provider.get_backend('ibmq_montreal')

noisy_sim = sim.from_backend(backend)

We cannot directly simulate the circuits as is because they are not mapped to the system. Here we will transpile them according to our Quick Start recipe where we do it multiple times and take the one with lowest cx gate count. Here I work only on the unbound circuit qc for simplicity:

trans_circs = transpile([qc]*10, backend, optimization_level=3, approximation_degree=0)
cx_counts = np.asarray([circ.count_ops().get('cx', 0) for circ in trans_circs])
print(cx_counts)
best_idx = np.where(cx_counts == np.min(cx_counts))[0][0]
trans_qc = trans_circs[best_idx]

[30 38 38 38 36 38 30 38 38 40]

We can see that there is a very wide difference in the number of cx gates between runs of the transpiler, and obviously we would like to avoid computing unneeded noisy gates if possible. Let us now run mapomatic and find a good set of qubits for this circuit to run on:

small_trans_qc = mm.deflate_circuit(trans_qc)
best_layout = mm.best_overall_layout(small_trans_qc, backend)[0]
best_trans_qc = mm.inflate_circuit(small_trans_qc, best_layout, backend)

We can now bind the parameters to this best circuit and do a noisy simulation on it:

best_trans_circs = [best_trans_qc.bind_parameters({g:val}) for val in g_vals]

noisy_counts = noisy_sim.run(best_trans_circs, shots=shots).result().get_counts()

noisy_expval = [-1 * cnts.get('1', 0)/shots + cnts.get('0', 0)/shots for cnts in noisy_counts]

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), ideal_expval, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='ideal')
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), noisy_expval, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='noisy')
ax.legend()
ax.set_xlabel('$g/2\pi$');

So we see that, according to the noisy simulation we did, we should get about 65% of the signal at \(g/2\pi=0.25\). This is ~3x better than the results from the paper, so lets run it for real and see.

Execution on actual hardware#

real_counts = backend.run(best_trans_circs, shots=shots).result().get_counts()

real_expval = [-1 * cnts.get('1', 0)/shots + cnts.get('0', 0)/shots for cnts in real_counts]

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), ideal_expval, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='ideal')
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), noisy_expval, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='noisy')
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), real_expval, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='real')
ax.legend()
ax.set_xlabel('$g/2\pi$');

Well crap. It turns out we get basically noise across the board. Did we do something wrong? Not really, it turns out that if you run the above on any IBM Quantum system you get noise! How the paper authors got 20% signal from their “raw” experiments is still a mystery to me. What is not a mystery is how to fix the problem, although it took a while to figure out.

Fixing the problem#

So we get junk on every system. Do we give up? No way. If you read the Quick Start guide you will note that so far we did not apply our dynamical decoupling (DD) step to this problem. That is where the solution is. If we look at Figure 1 posted at the top of the notebook you will notice that the last qubit, the one that is being measured, is sitting idle (just an empty wire) for the majority of the circuit duration. Thus that qubit, amongst others, is subject to dephasing unless we apply DD as an error suppression tool.

Our previous simulations did not pick that up because taking dephasing into account requires timing information in the circuit. To get that information we must “schedule” our circuits. Here we do “as-late-as-possible” scheduling, which is what the actual devices do:

sched_circs = transpile(best_trans_circs, backend, optimization_level=0, scheduling_method='alap')

Let us now plot the circuit for \(g/2\pi =0.25\)

sched_circs[5].draw('mpl')

It is a bit long, but you see that there are a large number of delay instructions in the circuit that enforce the timing of the operations in the circuit. The delay are all measured in dt, and looking closely one can see values that go up to ~60,000 dt, e.g. on qubit 19. How long is that in units of time? Well, we have to ask the backend what dt means in terms of seconds:

backend.configuration().dt

2.2222222222222221e-10

So the circuit has idle times that are up to

60e3 * backend.configuration().dt / 1e-6

13.333333333333334

microseconds. That is quite a long time for a qubit to be sitting around outside of the ground state. To see what the effect that these idle times have, we can perform another simulation on these scheduled circuits:

sched_counts = noisy_sim.run(sched_circs, shots=shots).result().get_counts()

sched_expval = [-1 * cnts.get('1', 0)/shots + cnts.get('0', 0)/shots for cnts in sched_counts]

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), ideal_expval, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='ideal')
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), noisy_expval, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='noisy')
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), real_expval, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='real')
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), sched_expval, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='scheduled-sim')
ax.legend()
ax.set_xlabel('$g/2\pi$');

So we see that idle times do play a big role in this problem. Even with the idle information the simulator still shows we should be doing much better then we do, but that is often the case with simulators; simulations always yield better results than what hardware is capable of. Let us now add our basic DD sequence from the Quick Start guide, and see if that helps.

durations = InstructionDurations.from_backend(backend)
constraints = backend.configuration().timing_constraints

dd_sequence = [XGate(), XGate()]
pm = PassManager([ALAPScheduleAnalysis(durations),
                  PadDynamicalDecoupling(durations, dd_sequence, pulse_alignment=constraints['pulse_alignment'])])
dd_circs = pm.run(best_trans_circs)

dd_circs[5].draw('mpl')

The original 60000 dt delay has now been broken into three pieces separated by x gates. In terms of time we have made the qubits active for only 0.5% of the original idle time. It doesn’t seem like it would help any but we can run the circuits on the system and find out:

dd_counts = backend.run(dd_circs, shots=shots).result().get_counts()

dd_expval = [-1 * cnts.get('1', 0)/shots + cnts.get('0', 0)/shots for cnts in dd_counts]

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), ideal_expval, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='ideal')
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), noisy_expval, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='noisy')
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), real_expval, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='real')
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), sched_expval, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='scheduled-sim')
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), dd_expval, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='real-DD')
ax.legend()
ax.set_xlabel('$g/2\pi$');

Ok, now we see the benefit of DD. We finally have something other than noise! Not as good as the simulations show, but again, this is not too surprising. We still do not have as good a result as the original authors did, but that may be because our circuit uses a reset instruction, where the original used a swap gate. On a Falcon R4 resets (measurements) are very long, and thus cause a host of issues. To find out if we can do better, let us move to a newer system.

Executing on a newer system#

As stated before, every system returns bad results for this problem unless DD is added. Let us see what we can do on a newer system following the steps above. Here we choose the ibm_peekskill Falcon R8 system. Amongst other things, it features coherence times that are ~3x longer than that of the ibmq_montreal system. So the effects of dephasing relaxation should be less. For brevity I will just do all the steps at once and produce the final plot.

backend2 = provider.get_backend('ibm_peekskill')

Find the best qubits on our new backend

small_trans_qc2 = mm.deflate_circuit(trans_qc)
best_layout2 = mm.best_overall_layout(small_trans_qc2, backend)[0]
best_trans_qc2 = mm.inflate_circuit(small_trans_qc2, best_layout, backend)

Bind parameters and do a noisy simulation

best_trans_circs2 = [best_trans_qc2.bind_parameters({g:val}) for val in g_vals]

noisy_sim2 = sim.from_backend(backend)

noisy_counts2 = noisy_sim2.run(best_trans_circs2, shots=shots).result().get_counts()

noisy_expval2 = [-1 * cnts.get('1', 0)/shots + cnts.get('0', 0)/shots for cnts in noisy_counts2]

Run on the backend

real_counts2 = backend2.run(best_trans_circs2, shots=shots).result().get_counts()

real_expval2 = [-1 * cnts.get('1', 0)/shots + cnts.get('0', 0)/shots for cnts in real_counts2]

Schedule circuits and apply DD

durations2 = InstructionDurations.from_backend(backend2)
constraints2 = backend2.configuration().timing_constraints

dd_sequence = [XGate(), XGate()]
pm = PassManager([ALAPScheduleAnalysis(durations2),
                  PadDynamicalDecoupling(durations2, dd_sequence, pulse_alignment=constraints2['pulse_alignment'])])
dd_circs2 = pm.run(best_trans_circs2)

sched_counts2 = noisy_sim2.run(dd_circs2, shots=shots).result().get_counts()

sched_expval2 = [-1 * cnts.get('1', 0)/shots + cnts.get('0', 0)/shots for cnts in sched_counts2]

Run DD circuits on backend

dd_counts2 = backend2.run(dd_circs2, shots=shots).result().get_counts()

dd_expval2 = [-1 * cnts.get('1', 0)/shots + cnts.get('0', 0)/shots for cnts in dd_counts2]

Plot the results

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), ideal_expval, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='ideal')
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), noisy_expval2, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='noisy')
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), real_expval2, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='real')
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), sched_expval2, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='scheduled-sim')
ax.plot(g_vals/(2*np.pi), dd_expval2, 'o--', ms=10, lw=1, label='real-DD')
ax.legend()
ax.set_xlabel('$g/2\pi$');

Excellent! From basically noise to about 65% of the full signal. In fact we get better than the scheduled circuit simulation because we applied dynamical decoupling. In fact, we have basically hit the noise limit of the original simulation without dephasing and relaxation included. Still not as good as the competition due to the limited connectivity and added swap gates, but we appear to have done our job. This example really highlights why dynamical decoupling is part of the basic recipe that one should try, especially if there are qubits that are idle for extended durations of time. We have also seen that it is possible to get more accurate simulations by scheduling circuits. This latter trick is not discussed much, so we hope readers find it useful.
